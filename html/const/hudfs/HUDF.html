In December 8, 2009, NASA's Hubble Space Telescope has made the deepest image of the universe ever taken in near-infrared light. The image was taken in the same region as the Hubble Ultra Deep Field (HUDF), which was taken in 2004 and is the deepest visible-light image of the universe. Hubble's newly installed Camera collects light from near-infrared wavelengths and therefore looks even deeper into the universe, because the light from very distant galaxies is stretched out of the ultraviolet and visible regions of the spectrum into near-infrared wavelengths by the expansion of the universe.
In September 25, 2012, astronomers made a better picture of humans deepest-ever picture of the universe, called the eXtreme Deep Field, or XDF, 10 years of NASA Hubble Space Telescope photographs taken of a patch of sky at the center of the original Hubble Ultra Deep Field made, the picture to the right. By collecting faint light over many hours of observation, it showed thousands of galaxies, both close and very far away, making it the deepest image of the universe ever taken at the time. The new full-color XDF image reaches much fainter galaxies and includes very deep exposures in red light from Hubble's new infrared camera. The XDF we can see about 5,500 galaxies. In 1996, astronamers did something amazing.   They pointed the Hubble Space Telescope into a part of the sky that they thought was empty.   This area was close to the Big Dipper. The patch of sky was no bigger than a grain of sand held out at arms length.

This was a somewhat risky move for the scientists.   Some questioned whether it would be wasted trying to look at nothing. There was a real risk that the images returned would be as black as the space at which it was being pointed.

They opened the telescope slowly, in 10 days, photons that had been travelling for over 13 billion light years finally ended their journey on most powerful telescope.   When the telescope was finally closed, the light from over 3,000 galaxies had covered the detector, making one of the best images in all of human history, each dot was an entire galaxy, each one containing hundreds of billions of stars.   In 2004, they did it again, this time pointing the telescope toward an area near the constellation Orion. They opened the shutter for over 11 days and 400 complete orbits around the Earth. Detectors with increased sensitivity and filters that allowed more light through than ever before allowed over 10,000 galaxies to appear in what became known as the Ultra Deep Field, an image that represented the farthest we've ever seen into the universe.
